# Sales Psychology & Influence Principles

## Overview

Final expense sales are emotional decisions, not logical ones. Prospects don't buy insurance — they buy peace of mind, freedom from worry, and the ability to protect their family. This file documents the psychological principles that underpin effective FE sales conversations.

---

## Core Principle: Emotion First, Logic Second

The decision to buy is almost always made emotionally. Logic is used afterward to justify the decision.

**What this means in practice:**
- Anchor on *who the prospect is protecting* before you ever mention price.
- Get the prospect to articulate their own worry — in their own words — before presenting a solution.
- Never start with features. Start with the question: "Who would be affected most if something happened to you?"

## The 6 Principles of Influence (Cialdini)

### 1. Reciprocity
People feel obligated to give back when they receive something first.

**In FE calls:**
- Provide genuine value before asking for anything (free information, a comparison, an honest assessment of their current coverage).
- Example: "Let me tell you what I can see from what you've described — you're actually paying more than you need to for less coverage than you think."

### 2. Commitment & Consistency
Once people commit to something small, they feel pressure to remain consistent with that commitment.

**In FE calls:**
- Use micro-commitments throughout the call: "Would you agree that having your family avoid any out-of-pocket costs is important to you?"
- Each "yes" builds momentum toward the final "yes."

### 3. Social Proof
People look to others for guidance on what to do — especially people they identify with.

**In FE calls:**
- Reference other clients in similar situations: "I worked with a lady just last week — she was 72, diabetic, and had been turned down before — and we were able to get her $15,000 in coverage for under $60 a month."
- Use specific, believable details (age, condition, amount) — not vague "many clients."

### 4. Authority
People follow the lead of credible experts.

**In FE calls:**
- Establish your expertise early without bragging: "I specialize in final expense — it's all I do, so I know these products inside and out."
- Reference carrier ratings, years in business, and state licensing where appropriate.

### 5. Liking
People buy from people they like. Liking is built through similarity, genuine interest, and humor.

**In FE calls:**
- Mirror the prospect's pace and energy level.
- Find genuine common ground — family, faith, region.
- Use the prospect's name naturally (not excessively).

### 6. Scarcity
People value what is rare or diminishing.

**In FE calls — use carefully and honestly:**
- Rate locks: "The premium I'm quoting you today is based on your current age. If we wait until after your birthday, the rate goes up."
- Health-based urgency: "The fact that you're in good health right now is actually an asset here — these plans are easier to qualify for when your health is good."

## Emotional Triggers in Final Expense Sales

### Fear of Burdening Family
The #1 emotional driver. Prospects do not want their death to cause financial hardship.

**How to surface it:**
> "If something were to happen to you unexpectedly, what would that look like for your family financially?"

> "Have you ever had to help pay for a family member's funeral? What was that experience like?"

### Guilt
Many seniors feel they haven't prepared adequately. Avoid inducing guilt — instead, relieve it.

> "The fact that you're taking this call and asking these questions tells me you care about your family. A lot of people put this off — you're already ahead of most."

### Love & Legacy
Prospects who frame coverage as a "gift" to their children are highly motivated.

> "A lot of my clients think of this as one final act of love — making sure their kids never have to worry about covering this cost."

### Peace of Mind
For many prospects, the relief of having a plan in place is the true product.

> "How would you feel knowing that this is handled — that no matter what happens, your family is taken care of?"

## The Power of Silence

Silence is one of the most underused tools in sales. After asking a powerful question — especially a closing question — **stop talking**.

- The first person to speak after a closing question typically loses.
- Silence allows the prospect to process and commit.
- Filling silence with more information actually reduces the chance of a close.

**Practice:** After "Would you like to go ahead and get this started today?" — put the phone down if you have to. Do not speak until they do.

## Anchoring

The first number mentioned in a negotiation becomes the reference point.

**In FE calls:**
- Anchor high on face amount before discussing premium: "Most of my clients in your situation go with $15,000 to $20,000."
- Anchor on funeral costs before giving premium: "Funerals run $12,000 to $15,000 on average now — how does $X/month compare to that?"

---

## Loss Aversion

People are more motivated by avoiding loss than by acquiring gain. They feel losses roughly twice as strongly as equivalent gains.

**Frame coverage in terms of what the family could lose without it:**
> "Without a plan like this in place, your family would have to come up with $12,000 or more — probably within 48 to 72 hours of the funeral."

**Not:**
> "With this plan, your family gets $12,000."

---

## The Ben Franklin Close (Balance Sheet)

When a prospect is on the fence, walk through a mental pros-and-cons list:

> "Let's think about this together. The reasons to have something like this in place are [list 3–4 benefits the prospect mentioned]. The only reason not to is [their stated objection]. Does that feel like a fair summary to you?"

---

## Mirroring & Pacing

Match the prospect's:
- **Speed** — slow down with slow-talking seniors; speed up slightly with impatient prospects
- **Vocabulary** — use their words, not insurance jargon
- **Energy level** — quiet and serious vs. warm and chatty

**Effect:** The prospect feels understood and "like you." This dramatically increases trust and close rates.

---

## Handling Emotional Objections vs. Logical Objections

| Type | Characteristic | Response Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Emotional | "I just don't know…" / "My daughter said…" | Acknowledge the feeling; don't argue facts |
| Logical | "I need to compare prices" / "I already have X" | Address the logic; provide data |
| Hybrid | "It seems expensive and I'm not sure if I need it" | Lead with emotion, follow with logic |

## Rapport Building in the First 60 Seconds

The first minute sets the tone for the entire call. Key principles:

1. **Match energy** — warm but not fake, professional but not cold.
2. **State purpose clearly** — never be vague about why you're calling.
3. **Ask before you tell** — get them talking before you start presenting.
4. **Use their name** — once at opening, once mid-call, once at close.

## The Assumptive Close Psychology

An assumptive close works because it shifts the default. Instead of asking "Do you want to move forward?" (which invites a no), you assume the answer is yes and ask about logistics.

> "Let me go ahead and get this started for you — is [name] spelled [spelling]?"

This isn't manipulation — if the prospect doesn't want to proceed, they'll say so. The assumptive close just removes the unnecessary friction of a binary yes/no question.

---

## Psychology of the Callback

Callbacks have a dramatically lower close rate than same-call closes. Every callback is a potential lost sale.

**The data:**
- Same call close rate: typically 40–70% (depending on lead quality and agent skill)
- Callback close rate: typically 10–25%

**Mindset shift:**
> You are not doing the prospect a favor by letting them "think about it." You are increasing the probability that they will procrastinate indefinitely and remain unprotected.

The most ethical close is a same-call close — because it ensures the family is protected.
