# Compliance Guide

## Overview

Final expense sales are regulated at the state level. Non-compliance can result in fines, license suspension, or civil liability. This file documents the universal do's and don'ts, state-specific rules, and required disclosures.

> **This document is not legal advice.** Consult your compliance officer and the carrier's compliance department for authoritative guidance. State regulations change — verify annually.

---

## Universal Rules (All States)

### Always Do
- [ ] Identify yourself by name and company name within the first 15 seconds
- [ ] State the purpose of the call clearly
- [ ] Disclose that you are an insurance agent or producer
- [ ] Provide your state license number upon request
- [ ] Explain the free-look period before issuing the policy
- [ ] Explain graded and modified benefits fully and clearly before the prospect agrees to the policy
- [ ] Obtain a signed or recorded authorization before processing an application
- [ ] Leave a callback number
- [ ] Honor all Do Not Call requests immediately

### Never Do
- [ ] Do not misrepresent policy benefits, exclusions, or costs
- [ ] Do not guarantee claim approval or claim payment timelines
- [ ] Do not suggest a policy is free, "just a formality," or doesn't require premium payment
- [ ] Do not claim a policy covers medical expenses if it does not
- [ ] Do not pressure a prospect with artificial deadlines (unless rate change is genuinely imminent)
- [ ] Do not make negative statements about a competitor's financial stability unless you can document them
- [ ] Do not recommend replacing existing coverage without a documented suitability analysis
- [ ] Do not call numbers on the National Do Not Call Registry without prior written consent
- [ ] Do not accept premium payments in cash unless explicitly permitted by the carrier
- [ ] Do not sign an application on behalf of a prospect
- [ ] Do not submit an application you know contains false health information

## Replacement Rules

A replacement occurs when a new policy is sold and existing coverage is lapsed, surrendered, or reduced as a result.

### Required Disclosures in Replacement
- Inform the prospect that the new policy will have a new contestability period (typically 2 years)
- Inform the prospect that a new waiting period may apply (for graded or modified plans)
- Provide a written comparison of the old and new policy (most states require this)
- Complete a Replacement Notice form and have the prospect sign it

### When Replacement Is Appropriate
- Existing coverage has increasing premiums (AARP, Colonial Penn term)
- Existing coverage will expire at a certain age
- Prospect is paying for less coverage than they need
- Existing coverage has better terms available elsewhere for the same or lower price

### When Replacement May Not Be Appropriate
- Existing policy is a permanent plan with significant cash value that would be lost
- New plan has a longer waiting period than the existing plan
- Prospect is in poor health and may not survive a new contestability period

## Suitability Standards

Before recommending a policy, confirm:

1. The premium is affordable relative to the prospect's fixed income
2. The death benefit is appropriate for the stated need (funeral and final expenses)
3. The prospect understands what they are buying and how it works
4. Replacement (if applicable) is in the prospect's best interest

**Suitability red flags:**
- Monthly premium exceeds 10–15% of the prospect's stated monthly income
- Prospect has cognitive impairment that affects decision-making capacity
- Prospect is being pressured by a third party to purchase coverage they don't want
- Prospect doesn't understand the policy after clear explanation

If suitability is in question, document your concerns and do not complete the application without consulting your upline or compliance officer.

## Senior Protection Rules

Many states have enacted enhanced protections for senior consumers (typically age 65+):

### Common Senior-Specific Requirements
- Extended free-look period (30 days in most states, vs. 10 days standard)
- Cooling-off period after application submission
- Prohibition on high-pressure sales tactics
- Requirement to involve a "trusted adult" in some circumstances

## Do Not Call (DNC) Compliance

### National DNC Registry
- Scrub call lists against the National DNC Registry every 31 days
- Established business relationship (EBR) exemption: can call existing customers for 18 months after last transaction
- Express written consent exemption: can call DNC-registered numbers with signed written consent

### State-Specific DNC Rules
Some states maintain their own DNC registries with stricter rules than the federal list.

### Internal DNC List

- Never call a number on your internal DNC list — this is separate from the federal registry

## Telemarketing Compliance (TCPA)

The Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA) governs how and when you can call prospects.

### Key TCPA Rules
- Do not call before 8 AM or after 9 PM in the prospect's local time zone
- Do not use auto-dialers without prior express written consent
- Provide opt-out mechanism during any prerecorded message
- Keep records of consent for at least 4 years

## Recording Calls

Recording requirements vary by state:

| Consent Type | States | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| One-party consent | Most states | Agent may record without disclosing |
| Two-party (all-party) consent | CA, CT, FL, IL, MD, MA, MI, NH, OR, PA, WA | Must disclose recording to prospect before recording begins |

**Best practice:** Always disclose at the start of a call that it may be recorded for quality assurance, regardless of state. This protects you and builds trust.

> "Just so you know, this call may be recorded for quality assurance purposes."

## Contestability Period

All life insurance policies have a 2-year contestability period from the date of issue. During this period, the carrier can:
- Investigate the cause of death
- Review the application for misrepresentations
- Deny a claim if material misrepresentation is found on the application

**Why this matters for agents:**
- Never encourage a prospect to omit or misrepresent health conditions on an application
- If a claim is denied due to misrepresentation, the agent may face professional and legal consequences
- Explain the contestability period to every prospect so they understand why honest answers matter

## Anti-Money Laundering (AML)

Agents must complete AML training annually. Key obligations:

- Identify and verify the identity of applicants
- Do not accept unusually large cash premium payments
- Report suspicious activity to your upline and/or carrier
- Know the signs of insurance fraud (straw purchases, rapid policy loans, etc.)
