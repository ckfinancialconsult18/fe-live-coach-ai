# Discovery Questions

## Overview

Discovery is the most important part of a final expense call. Agents who skip discovery and go straight to a pitch close at a fraction of the rate of agents who invest 5–10 minutes in genuine discovery. Questions serve three purposes:

1. **Surface need** — help the prospect articulate their own worry in their own words
2. **Qualify the prospect** — gather underwriting, coverage, and budget information
3. **Build trust** — people trust people who listen

---

## The Discovery Framework

Discovery should flow in this order:

```
Permission → Personal Connection → Existing Situation → Emotional Need → Health → Budget
```

Never jump to health or budget before you've established personal connection and surfaced the emotional need. Asking "how much do you weigh?" in the first two minutes destroys rapport.

---

## 1. Permission Questions

Get permission to ask questions before diving in. This sets an expectation of dialogue, not a pitch.

> "Before I share some information with you, do you mind if I ask a few questions to make sure I'm showing you the right plan?"

> "I want to make sure whatever I show you is actually a good fit — can I ask you a couple of quick questions first?"

---

## 2. Rapport and Connection Questions

These questions create a human connection before the sales conversation begins.

- "How's the weather down there in [city]?" *(light opener)*
- "Have you lived there long?"
- "Are you originally from [state]?"
- "Do you have family nearby?"
- "Are you retired, or are you still working?"

**Key rule:** Listen genuinely. React to what they say. If they mention grandkids, ask about them. If they mention health issues, acknowledge them with empathy before moving forward.

---

## 3. Existing Coverage Questions

Surface what they already have before presenting anything new.

- "Do you currently have any life insurance in place?"
- "What company is that through — is it something through your employer, or is it a policy you own personally?"
- "Do you know approximately how much coverage you have?"
- "Do you know what your monthly premium is on that?"
- "Is that coverage guaranteed to stay in place no matter what, or could it ever increase in cost or expire?"
- "Has anyone reviewed that policy with you recently to make sure it's still the right fit?"

**What to listen for:**
- Group/employer coverage → may lapse at retirement; not permanent
- AARP, Colonial Penn → likely increasing premiums; may be insufficient
- "I think I have X" (uncertainty) → they haven't reviewed it recently

---

## 4. Emotional Need Questions

These are the most important questions on the call. Get the prospect to articulate their own motivation in their own words.

### Opening the Need
- "What was it that made you fill out the card and request this information?"
- "What made you decide now was a good time to look into something like this?"
- "Had you been thinking about this for a while, or was there something specific that prompted it?"

### The Burden Question (Most Powerful)
> "If something were to happen to you, what would that look like financially for your family?"

> "Do you have family who would be responsible for handling arrangements if something happened to you?"

> "Would those costs fall on your children, or do you have something in place to cover that?"

### The Legacy Question
- "Is leaving something behind for your family important to you?"
- "When you think about the coverage you want, is it just to cover final costs, or is it to leave something extra for your [children/grandchildren]?"

### The Experience Question
- "Have you ever had to help cover the cost of a funeral for a family member?"
- "Do you know what funerals cost these days?"

**What to listen for:**
- "I don't want to be a burden" → strongest buying signal; remember this and reference it in the close
- A personal story about a family member's death → powerful emotional anchor
- "My kids can't afford it" → specific fear; address it directly

---

## 5. Health Discovery Questions

Move to health after emotional need is established. Frame health questions as being in the prospect's interest.

> "Now, I want to find you the plan that's going to give you the best rate and the best coverage — so I need to ask a few health questions. Is that okay?"

### Age and Build
- "Can I ask how old you are?"
- "And can I ask your height and approximate weight? I want to make sure I'm pulling the right rates."

### Tobacco
- "Are you a tobacco user — cigarettes, cigars, or any nicotine products?"
- *(If yes)* "How long have you been a smoker? And have you tried to quit in the past year?"
- *(If quit)* "When did you quit? Was it more or less than a year ago?"

### Health Conditions (Open → Specific)
Start open:
> "Besides any medications you're taking, have you had any major health conditions in the past few years?"

Then probe specifically:
- "Do you have any heart-related conditions — heart attack, heart failure, irregular heartbeat?"
- "Have you ever had a stroke or a TIA?"
- "Do you have diabetes? Is that managed with pills or insulin?"
- "Do you have any lung conditions — COPD, emphysema, or asthma?"
- "Have you ever been diagnosed with cancer? Are you currently being treated, or is that behind you?"
- "Do you have any kidney problems?"
- "Do you use any assistive devices — a walker, wheelchair, or oxygen?"

### Hospitalization
- "Have you been hospitalized or had any surgeries in the past couple of years?"

---

## 6. Budget Discovery Questions

Budget is last — after need and value are established.

**Never ask "what's your budget?" before anchoring on what coverage costs.** Instead, anchor on funeral cost first.

> "Just so you have a reference point — funerals today average between $10,000 and $15,000 when you factor in everything. Is covering that cost the main goal, or were you thinking about leaving more than that?"

Then:
> "What were you hoping to spend on something like this each month? Just so I know what range to work in."

Or:
> "Most of my clients in your situation end up in the $30 to $60 a month range — does that seem workable for you, or were you thinking lower than that?"

**What to listen for:**
- A specific number → design the plan around it
- "I don't know" → offer a range anchor as above
- "As low as possible" → they may be more budget-conscious than need-conscious; reinforce value before presenting price

---

## 7. Commitment Confirmation Questions (Pre-Close Discovery)

Before closing, verify the prospect is emotionally and logistically ready:

- "So just to make sure I've got this right — the main thing you're trying to do here is make sure [family member] doesn't have to come out of pocket for anything if something happens to you. Is that right?"
- "And the $[X]/month fits your budget?"
- "Is there anyone else you'd want to talk to before we get this started, or is this a decision you can make on your own today?"
- "Is there anything else I need to know before I put this together for you?"

---

## Questions That Kill Rapport (Do Not Use)

| Question | Why to Avoid |
|---|---|
| "Are you healthy?" | Too vague; closes down the conversation |
| "Do you have any pre-existing conditions?" | Sounds clinical; use condition-specific questions instead |
| "Why are you just now looking at this?" | Judgmental; creates defensiveness |
| "What's the name of your bank?" *(too early)* | Premature; only after commitment to buy |
| "How much do you make?" | Too personal; ask about what they pay currently instead |

## Question Sequencing Cheat Sheet

```
CALL START
    ↓
Warm opener (location, family, weather)
    ↓
"Before I share anything, can I ask a few quick questions?"
    ↓
Existing coverage → What do you have? How much? What company?
    ↓
"What made you fill out the card?" → surface the emotional need
    ↓
"If something happened, what would that look like for your family?"
    ↓
Health questions (age → tobacco → conditions → mobility → hospitalizations)
    ↓
"What were you hoping to spend each month?" → budget
    ↓
Trial close → Present solution
    ↓
CLOSE
```
