# Closing Scripts

## Overview

Closing is not manipulation — it is the moment you help the prospect take the action they already want to take. The scripts in this file are designed to be natural, pressure-free, and effective. Adapt the words to your voice; don't recite them robotically.

---

## The #1 Rule of Closing

**Ask the closing question. Then stop talking.**

Silence after a close is not awkward — it is necessary. The prospect is processing. If you fill the silence with more information, you give them a reason to say "I need to think about it." Wait for their answer.

---

## Trial Closes (Use Throughout the Call)

Trial closes are small commitment questions used during discovery and presentation to test the prospect's interest and build momentum toward the final close.

**Examples:**
- "Does that make sense so far?"
- "Is that the kind of thing you're looking for?"
- "Can you see how that would give your family peace of mind?"
- "If the price was right, is this something you'd want to move forward with?"
- "If we could get you that amount of coverage for around [budget they mentioned], would that work for you?"

**Rule:** Use one trial close after each major benefit statement. Never use more than one at a time.

---

## The Assumptive Close

The assumptive close treats the decision as already made and moves directly to logistics. It is the most powerful and least pushy way to close.

**Standard Assumptive Close:**
> "Let me go ahead and get this started for you. I just need to verify a few things — is your last name spelled [spelling]?"

**Benefit Recap + Assumptive Close:**
> "So based on everything you've shared with me, I think [plan] at [$XX]/month gives you the best protection for your family. Let's go ahead and get this taken care of — is your address still [address from lead]?"

**Age-Urgency Assumptive Close:**
> "Your current age actually gives us the best available rate right now. Let me lock that in for you — what's the best mailing address for the policy documents?"

---

## The Summary Close

Recap the prospect's stated needs and desires, then tie the product directly to each one before closing.

> "Let me make sure I have this right. You told me you have about $5,000 with AARP, but you're worried that's not enough to cover a funeral today. You want your kids to be able to handle everything without having to scramble for money. And you want something where the premium won't go up on you. Is that right?
>
> [Pause for confirmation]
>
> Perfect. What I'm showing you here covers all three of those things — $15,000 in permanent coverage, a locked premium of [$XX]/month, and it goes into effect immediately. Let's get this in place for you today."

---

## The Either/Or Close

Offer two options rather than asking a yes/no question. Either answer moves the conversation forward.

> "The $10,000 plan comes out to [$XX]/month, and the $15,000 plan is [$XX]/month. Which of those fits your budget better?"

> "I can set the premium payment up on the 1st or the 15th of the month — which one lines up better with when you get your check?"

---

## The "Protect Your Family" Emotional Close

Use when the prospect has expressed concern about being a burden or leaving their family with bills.

> "You mentioned earlier that you don't want [name] to have to worry about this. The only way to make sure that happens is to get this in place today. You've already done the hard part — you called, you answered the questions, you know what you need. Let's go ahead and finish this so you can have that peace of mind."

---

## The Urgency Close (Rate Lock)

Use when the prospect's birthday is approaching, when rates are genuinely about to change, or when they've been procrastinating.

> "The quote I've given you today is based on your current age. If we wait until after [month] when you turn [age], the premium goes up by about [$X]/month — and it stays there permanently. So the cheapest day to lock this in is today. Let me get this started for you."

---

## The "What Would It Take" Close

Use when a prospect is hesitant but engaged — probe for the specific thing standing in the way.

> "Can I ask you a straightforward question? What would it take for you to feel comfortable moving forward with this today?"

Listen carefully to the answer — it tells you exactly what objection to handle.

---

## The Third-Party Validation Close

Use when a prospect needs reassurance from an external authority.

> "You're not the first person I've talked to who felt this way. What I can tell you is that [Carrier] has been in business for [X] years, they have an A rating with A.M. Best, and they pay the vast majority of claims within [timeframe]. Your family will be protected — I promise you that."

---

## The "Future Pain" Close

Paint a picture of what happens if they don't act today.

> "Here's what I want you to think about. If something were to happen to you tomorrow, what would [daughter's name / spouse] have to do? She'd have to call a funeral home, make arrangements — and write a check for $12,000 or more — probably within 48 hours. That's a hard position to put someone you love in.
>
> This plan eliminates that completely. For [$XX] a month, you take that weight off her permanently. Doesn't that seem worth it?"

---

## The Callback Close (When You Can't Same-Call Close)

When the prospect insists on calling back, set a specific appointment — not an open-ended "call me next week."

> "I completely understand. I want you to take your time with this. Can we set a specific time? I have [Day] at [Time] or [Day] at [Time] — which works better for you?"

Then confirm:
> "Perfect. I'll call you [Day] at [Time]. I'll have everything ready so we can get this wrapped up quickly. Does [number] ring through to you directly?"

---

## Handling "I'm Ready to Go Ahead" (The Confirmation Close)

When a prospect says yes, confirm quickly and professionally before they change their mind:

> "Wonderful — I'm so glad we were able to get this taken care of for you. Let me grab just a few more pieces of information and we'll have this all set up. I'll need your [SSN / banking info / beneficiary name]…"

Move quickly. Do not re-explain the policy. Do not give them a reason to second-guess. Get the application submitted.

---

## Post-Close Reassurance

Immediately after submission, reinforce the decision:

> "You just made a great decision. Your family is going to have [face amount] in protection, your premium is locked in at [$XX], and it can never be cancelled as long as you pay the premium. You'll receive the policy in the mail in about [X] weeks. Is there anything else I can help you with?"

---

## Close Failure Recovery

If the prospect says no at the close:

**Step 1:** Don't panic. Ask what's holding them back.
> "I understand. Can I ask — is there something specific that's making you hesitant?"

**Step 2:** Listen without interrupting. Address the specific concern.

**Step 3:** If they still won't commit, offer a callback with a specific time, not an open-ended one.

**Step 4:** If they say absolute no, thank them graciously.
> "I completely respect that. If you ever change your mind or your situation changes, I'd love to help you. Would it be okay if I followed up in a few months just to check in?"
