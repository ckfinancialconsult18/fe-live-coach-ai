# Buying Signals

## Overview

A buying signal is any verbal or behavioral cue that indicates the prospect is emotionally or logically moving toward a purchase decision. Missing a buying signal is one of the most common mistakes in FE sales — the agent keeps pitching when they should be closing.

**Rule:** When you detect a buying signal, stop presenting and move toward a trial close or assumptive close. Do not continue to sell someone who is already ready to buy.

---

## Signal Strength Classification

| Strength | Meaning | Response |
|---|---|---|
| 🟢 Strong | Prospect is close to or ready to commit | Move directly to assumptive close |
| 🟡 Medium | Prospect is engaged and open; needs one more nudge | Trial close; address any remaining concern |
| 🔵 Weak | Prospect is listening but not yet there | Continue discovery; deepen emotional need |

---

## Verbal Buying Signals

### 🟢 Strong Signals

**"I don't want to be a burden on my kids."**
This is the #1 buying signal in final expense. The prospect has already internalized the need. Stop selling. Close.

> **Response:** "That's exactly what this plan takes care of. Let me go ahead and get this started for you."

---

**"How much would that cost?"**
A price question means they're imagining having it. Do not answer with a number immediately — anchor value first.

> **Response:** "Great question. Before I give you a number, let me make sure I'm showing you the right coverage amount. You mentioned you'd want at least $[X] — is that still the range you're thinking?"

---

**"That sounds too good to be true."**
This sounds like skepticism, but it's actually an expression of desire. They *want* it to be true.

> **Response:** "I understand — a lot of my clients say the same thing at first. Here's what makes it possible: [brief explanation]. Does that make it make sense?"

---

**"My neighbor just passed and the family had to pay for everything."**
A recent loss experience creates urgency and emotional resonance. The prospect is thinking about their own mortality.

> **Response:** "I'm sorry to hear that. That's exactly the situation we're trying to prevent for your family. Let me show you what we can put in place so that never happens to them."

---

**"I've been meaning to do this for a while."**
They've been thinking about it — you're not introducing the idea. They just needed someone to help them act.

> **Response:** "You're not alone in that — a lot of people put it off. The good news is you're taking care of it today. Let's get this locked in for you."

---

**"What happens if I pass away in the first year?"**
They're imagining being a policyholder. This is a forward-looking question — they're in.

> **Response:** Explain the policy terms (level = full benefit day one; graded = return of premiums), then: "Once I explain that, does it still seem like the right fit for you?" Then close.

---

**"Do you need my banking information?"**
They are ready to buy. Do not keep presenting.

> **Response:** "Yes, and I'll ask for that in just a moment — let me just grab a couple more pieces of information first." Then close immediately after.

---

### 🟡 Medium Signals

**"That makes sense."**
They're following and agreeing. Trial close opportunity.

> **Response:** "Good — does it seem like something you'd want to move forward with?"

---

**"How long does this take?"**
They're mentally allocating time for the process — they're in.

> **Response:** "It's pretty quick — about 10 minutes to get the application submitted, and you'll have a policy number the same day."

---

**"What does my [spouse/daughter/son] need to do?"**
They're thinking through logistics with a third party involved — engagement is high.

> **Response:** "For the application, I just need your information. Your [family member] would be listed as the beneficiary. Would it help if I gave them a quick call to answer any questions they have?"

---

**"I have a checkup coming up in a few weeks — should I wait?"**
They're planning to buy — just negotiating timing.

> **Response:** "The good news is we don't need a medical exam or a doctor's visit for this — it's based on the health questions we've already gone through. There's no reason to wait, and locking in your rate now means you're getting it at your current age."

---

**"What if I change my mind?"**
They're looking for reassurance, not an exit. Close, then reassure.

> **Response:** Close first: "Let's get this started." Then: "And just so you know, every policy comes with a 30-day free look period. If you change your mind after you receive it, you can send it back for a full refund — no questions asked."

---

**"Is this one of those plans where the price goes up?"**
They've had a bad experience with increasing premiums. Address directly, then close.

> **Response:** "No — this is whole life insurance. The premium is locked in permanently the day it's issued. It can never go up, no matter how old you get or how your health changes."

---

### 🔵 Weak Signals (Engagement, Not Yet Commitment)

**"Tell me more about that."**
Good engagement. Continue; don't close yet.

**"I've seen ads for something like this."**
Awareness exists. Differentiate from what they've seen (usually Colonial Penn or AARP), then build toward close.

**"That's interesting."**
Mild engagement. Surface emotional need before presenting more product details.

## Non-Verbal and Tonal Buying Signals

These apply on phone calls where vocal cues replace body language:

| Cue | Signal | Response |
|---|---|---|
| Pace slows down | They're reflecting | Match the slower pace; allow silence |
| Tone becomes warmer | Emotional connection established | Mirror warmth; move toward emotional close |
| They start asking logistical questions | Ready to commit | Begin close |
| They laugh and relax | Trust established | Trial close |
| Silence after you present the price | Processing, not resistance | Wait. Do not fill the silence. |
| They start repeating back what you said | Confirming understanding | Validate and move to close |

---

## False Buying Signals (Watch These)

Not every positive cue means they're ready to buy. Watch for these misleading signals:

| Signal | What It Looks Like | What It Really Means |
|---|---|---|
| "Sounds good" (from an Agreeable profile) | Enthusiasm | Politeness; verify before closing |
| "I'll definitely call you back" | Commitment | Almost always avoidance |
| "This is exactly what I need" + "but let me think" | Strong interest + stall | One unresolved objection; find and address it |
| Asking about a specific feature in detail | Deep interest | Maybe just curiosity; probe for readiness |

---

## The Signal → Close Sequence

When you detect a strong buying signal, follow this three-step sequence:

1. **Acknowledge the signal naturally** — don't announce "great, that's a buying signal"
2. **Ask one confirming question** — make sure the signal is genuine
3. **Move to assumptive or trial close** — don't delay

**Example:**

Prospect: "I really don't want my kids to have to deal with all of this."

Agent: "I completely understand that. A lot of my clients feel the same way. [step 1] If we could take that completely off the table for them, would you want to get this started today?" [step 2 — trial close]

Prospect: "Yeah, I think I would."

Agent: "Perfect. Let me go ahead and get this put together for you — is your address still [address]?" [step 3 — assumptive close]
