# Prospect Personality Profiles

## Overview

Different prospects require different communication styles. Recognizing personality type early in the call allows you to adapt your pace, language, and approach to build maximum rapport and reduce resistance. This guide uses a simplified DISC-adjacent model adapted for FE sales.

---

## The Four Prospect Profiles

| Profile | Core Driver | Biggest Fear | Responds To | Pace |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| The Protector | Family / legacy | Loved ones suffering | Emotional stories, burden imagery | Warm, unhurried |
| The Skeptic | Control / facts | Being fooled or pressured | Data, credentials, slow build | Patient, methodical |
| The Decisive | Efficiency | Wasting time | Bottom line, speed, clear options | Fast, direct |
| The Agreeable | Harmony | Conflict | Validation, reassurance, gentle pace | Warm, gentle |

---

## Profile 1: The Protector

### Who They Are
The Protector's primary motivation is family. They often mention grandchildren, worry about their children's financial burden, and have deeply felt emotional concerns about being a burden. They've usually been thinking about this for a while.

### How to Identify
- Volunteers family information early ("I have three grandkids")
- Uses phrases like "I don't want to leave them with bills"
- May have experienced the death of a peer or family member recently
- Warm, relational tone from the first minute

### Adapting Your Style
- Lead with the emotional need, not the product
- Personalize: use family member names when they share them
- Tell stories about other clients in similar situations (with anonymized details)
- Take your time — rushing feels disrespectful to this profile
- Mirror their language: if they say "peace of mind," use that phrase back

### Close That Works Best
The "Protect Your Family" emotional close and the Summary Close.

> "You mentioned you don't want [daughter's name] to have to worry about this. Getting this in place today is the one thing you can do right now to make sure that never happens."

### What to Avoid
- Moving too fast to price
- Sounding like you're "doing a transaction"
- Talking over their stories about family

## Profile 2: The Skeptic

### Who They Are
The Skeptic has been misled before — by an insurance agent, a contractor, a financial advisor, or simply by life. They are not hostile, but they test everything. They will ask hard questions, probe for inconsistencies, and take their time. Respect them for it.

### How to Identify
- Asks pointed questions early ("What company is this?", "What are you selling me?")
- May reference a bad experience with insurance in the past
- Cautious tone; does not volunteer personal information easily
- May say "I don't trust insurance companies"

### Adapting Your Style
- Be transparent first: state exactly who you are and why you're calling
- Acknowledge their skepticism directly: "That's a fair concern — let me show you exactly how this works"
- Use specific data: carrier ratings, years in business, claim statistics
- Don't push — invite them to ask more questions
- Slow down. Every implication of pressure will confirm their fears.
- Follow up every claim with proof: "And I can show you that in writing when you receive the policy"

### Close That Works Best
The Third-Party Validation Close and evidence-based summary.

> "You're right to ask that. [Carrier] has been in business since [year], they hold an A rating from A.M. Best — that's the gold standard for insurance financial strength — and their average claim is paid within [timeframe]. You're in good hands."

### What to Avoid
- Moving to close before they're ready
- Using vague language like "a lot of our clients" — be specific
- Sounding defensive when they push back
- Dismissing their objections

## Profile 3: The Decisive

### Who They Are
The Decisive prospect is busy, direct, and has no patience for small talk or rambling. They make decisions quickly — but they expect you to respect their time. If you give them a clear, compelling bottom line fast, they'll close fast. If you take 10 minutes to get to the point, they'll end the call.

### How to Identify
- Immediately asks "What is this about?" or "How much does it cost?"
- Short answers; doesn't volunteer information
- May sound impatient or brisk
- May try to rush you: "Just give me the bottom line"

### Adapting Your Style
- Be crisp. State who you are and why you're calling in the first 20 seconds.
- Ask 2–3 key discovery questions — don't ask 10
- Get to the price faster than you normally would (but still after anchoring value once)
- Present one clear recommendation, not multiple options
- Don't over-explain — present, ask for the close, and stop

### Close That Works Best
The Assumptive Close or Either/Or Close.

> "Based on what you've told me, the $15,000 plan at [$XX]/month is the right fit. Let me get that started for you — what's the best address for the policy?"

### What to Avoid
- Long stories or analogies
- Emotional appeals that feel manipulative to a fact-focused person
- Filler phrases ("So what I can do for you is…", "Great question…")
- Asking the same question twice

## Profile 4: The Agreeable

### Who They Are
The Agreeable prospect is warm, polite, and will say yes to almost anything on the surface — including yes to calling back, yes to thinking about it, and yes to your close, even when they don't fully mean it. Their "yes" needs to be verified. They avoid conflict and need reassurance more than persuasion.

### How to Identify
- Very polite, easy to talk to
- Says "sure" and "sounds good" frequently
- May agree with everything but remain non-committal
- Avoids saying "no" directly — will often say "let me think about it" instead
- Often defers to family: "I'd have to check with my son"

### Adapting Your Style
- Be warm and unhurried — match their energy
- Build genuine rapport before getting into business
- Confirm their true feelings rather than accepting surface agreement: "When you say that sounds good, do you feel ready to move forward, or is there something still holding you back?"
- Give them permission to say no — paradoxically, this makes them more likely to say yes: "I want to make sure this is right for you — if it's not, just tell me"
- Involve the third party proactively rather than letting it be an escape route

### Close That Works Best
A gentle assumptive close with immediate reassurance.

> "Let's go ahead and get this set up — I think you'll feel great once it's in place. I'll walk you through everything step by step."

Followed immediately by:
> "And remember, you have a full 30 days to review the policy once you receive it. If anything doesn't look right, you can send it back for a full refund. You're completely protected."

### What to Avoid
- Taking their verbal agreement at face value — verify it
- Letting the "need to talk to my son" objection go unaddressed
- Applying any pressure — it will cause them to retreat into politeness and ultimately decline

## Mixed Profiles

Most prospects are a blend of two profiles. Common combinations:

| Blend | Description | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Protector + Agreeable | Warm, family-focused, non-committal | Lead emotionally; involve family member proactively |
| Skeptic + Decisive | Wants fast answers but will verify everything | Be crisp AND specific; have data ready |
| Protector + Skeptic | Cares deeply but has been burned before | Earn trust slowly; story-tell; don't rush |
| Decisive + Agreeable | Outwardly quick but avoids actual decisions | Get verbal confirmation before moving forward |

## Vocal Cues for Profile Identification

| Cue | Likely Profile |
|---|---|
| Mentions family member by name unprompted | Protector |
| Asks "What company are you with?" immediately | Skeptic |
| Says "Just give me the short version" | Decisive |
| Laughs and agrees with everything | Agreeable |
| Speaks slowly and deliberately | Skeptic or Protector |
| Speaks quickly and interrupts | Decisive |
| Soft voice, many "okays" and "sures" | Agreeable |
